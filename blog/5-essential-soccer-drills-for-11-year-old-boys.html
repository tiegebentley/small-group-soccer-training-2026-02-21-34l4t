5 Essential Soccer Drills for 11 Year Old Boys

March 15, 2026 Coach Marcus 8 min read

At age 11, boys are at a critical development stage for soccer. Their bodies are growing, their coordination is improving, and they're ready to tackle more complex drills. Here are 5 essential drills I use in my small group training sessions in Raleigh, NC that dramatically improve technique, speed, and confidence.

1. Cone Weaving with Speed Variations

What it develops: Close ball control, quick feet, change of pace, body feints

Setup: Place 8-10 cones in a straight line, 2 feet apart. Players dribble through the cones using both feet, alternating between inside and outside touches.

The key variation for 11 year olds: Start at 50% speed for the first pass. Second pass at 75%. Third pass at full game speed. This teaches them to control the ball at different paces—crucial for real match situations.

Coach's Tip:

Challenge players to keep their head up between cones. This simulates scanning for teammates during matches.

2. Triangle Passing Under Pressure

What it develops: First touch, passing accuracy, awareness, decision-making under pressure

Setup: Three players form a triangle (15 feet apart). One ball, continuous passing. Add a defender in the middle who applies passive pressure.

Why this works for 11 year olds: At this age, boys need to learn that soccer isn't just about having the ball—it's about what you do before you receive it. This drill forces them to check their shoulders, create passing angles, and receive the ball on the back foot.

Progression: Start with two-touch (control + pass). Progress to one-touch when they're ready. The defender increases pressure gradually.

3. 1v1 to Small Goals (Attacking & Defending)

What it develops: Confidence on the ball, defensive positioning, recovery runs, competitive mindset

Setup: Use two small goals (or cones) 20 yards apart. One attacker, one defender. Attacker starts with the ball and tries to score. After each round, they switch roles.

The mental component: Eleven year old boys respond incredibly well to competition. This drill taps into that competitive drive while teaching both attacking moves (step-overs, cuts, acceleration) and defensive fundamentals (jockeying, body positioning, timing).

Why This Works:

In small group training, I can give immediate feedback after each 1v1. "Great fake left, but keep the ball closer when you cut." That instant coaching makes all the difference.

4. Shooting Drill: First Touch Preparation

What it develops: Shooting technique, first touch into space, finishing under pressure

Setup: Player stands 20 yards from goal. Coach passes ball from the side. Player takes one touch to set up the shot, then shoots with second touch.

Critical coaching point: The first touch is everything. Teach them to take that first touch across their body (out of their feet) to create the shooting angle. Most 11 year olds want to shoot immediately—this drill teaches patience and preparation.

Variation: Add a passive defender who closes down after the first touch. This creates game-realistic pressure.

5. Small-Sided Game: 3v3 or 4v4

What it develops: Everything—game intelligence, positioning, teamwork, decision-making, stamina

Setup: Small field (30x20 yards), small goals, 3-4 players per side. Play 5-minute games with 1-minute rest between.

Why small-sided games are crucial for 11 year olds: In an 11v11 game, a player might touch the ball 10-15 times. In a 3v3 or 4v4 game during practice, they'll touch it 50-60 times. More touches = faster development.

The Small Group Advantage:

With only 6-8 boys in my training groups, every player is constantly involved. There's no standing around, no waiting in line. Every minute is high-quality touches on the ball.

How We Structure Training Sessions

A typical 60-minute session in my Raleigh small group training program looks like this:

  • Warm-up (10 min): Dynamic stretching + ball mastery
  • Technical drill (15 min): One of drills #1, #2, or #4 above
  • 1v1 or 2v2 (15 min): Competitive, game-realistic scenarios
  • Small-sided game (15 min): 3v3 or 4v4 to apply skills
  • Cool-down (5 min): Juggling challenge or light passing

The Benefits of Small Group Training

These drills work even better in small group settings because:

  • Individual attention: I can correct technique immediately
  • High repetition: More touches per player in every drill
  • Age-appropriate intensity: I adjust based on each boy's fitness level
  • Confidence building: Smaller groups mean less intimidation, more willingness to try new skills
  • Skill matching: I can pair similar ability levels for optimal development

What Parents Notice After 4-6 Weeks

When boys consistently work through these drills in small group training, parents typically see:

  • Better first touch and ball control
  • More confidence taking on defenders in games
  • Improved awareness (head up more often)
  • Faster decision-making
  • Better positioning without the ball
  • Increased enjoyment of the game
